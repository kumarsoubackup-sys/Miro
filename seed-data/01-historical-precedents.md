# Fuel Subsidy Removal: Historical Precedents and Political Consequences

## Overview

Between 2006 and 2019, efforts to reduce or eliminate gasoline subsidies led to protests in at least 24 countries. History shows that removal of subsidies is socially and politically difficult, with multiple countries enacting and then reversing reforms due to political backlash or social unrest.

## Nigeria — 2012 "Occupy Nigeria" Protests

On January 1, 2012, President Goodluck Jonathan abruptly removed the fuel subsidy with no prior warning. Demonstrations began on January 2 in Lagos, Abuja, and Kano. By January 6, protests had taken place in every major Nigerian city, with nearly all economic activity stopped in Lagos, Ibadan, and Kano.

The campaign became known as "Occupy Nigeria," referencing international protest movements. Participants included unions, students, medical and legal professionals, and notable figures like author Chimamanda Adichie.

Key events:
- January 1: Subsidy removal announced; fuel price doubled overnight
- January 2: Spontaneous demonstrations in major cities
- January 5: Police beat protesters; 44 injured
- January 9: National general strike commences; 11 total deaths reported
- January 16: Government partially reinstates subsidy; fuel price reduced from 141 to 97 Naira per liter (original was 65 Naira)

Government response: President Jonathan announced a supervisory panel, 25% salary reduction for officials, and increased bus services. He maintained military support but refused to reinstate full subsidies. Oil production nearly ceased, threatening Nigeria's economy which depends 90% on oil exports.

Social media played a significant role: Facebook groups like "Nationwide Anti-Fuel Subsidy Removal: Strategies & Protests" gained over 20,000 members within days. Twitter became a primary coordination tool for protesters.

## Nigeria — 2023 Subsidy Removal

President Tinubu announced the end of fuel subsidies on May 29, 2023. The cost of petrol skyrocketed by 300% to 650 Naira per liter within days. The subsidies had cost Nigeria approximately $10 billion in 2022, consuming about 40% of national revenue.

A public opinion poll revealed 73% of Nigerians were dissatisfied with the manner of removal. Inflation reached approximately 40%. Essential food prices increased over 150% annually. Rental costs surged 70-100%. Major multinational corporations (GlaxoSmithKline, Procter & Gamble, Shoprite) ceased Nigerian operations entirely. Leading companies incurred over $500 million in foreign exchange losses.

In August 2024, widespread hunger protests led by young people erupted across Lagos, Kano, Kaduna, Ibadan, Benin, and Port Harcourt. Security forces used tear gas and arrested protesters. Analysts warned that "the next protest may be spontaneous, possibly leading to chaos and regime change, as witnessed in Bangladesh."

## Indonesia — 1998 and 2022

In 1997-98, Indonesia removed fuel subsidies after the Asian financial crisis. A 71% gasoline price increase damaged President Soeharto's credibility and ignited violent protests lasting weeks, ultimately forcing his resignation after 31 years in power.

In 2022, President Joko Widodo announced a 30% fuel price increase after government energy spending ballooned threefold to AU$50 billion. Thousands of protesters surrounded Jakarta's Presidential Palace. Demonstrations erupted across regional cities including Bengkulu and Yogyakarta. Student and labor groups organized demonstrations for over a week.

The government implemented direct cash transfers targeting 20+ million households and wage subsidies for earners below IDR 3.5 million monthly. A key lesson: previous presidents delayed subsidy cuts until their final terms to avoid electoral backlash.

## Bolivia — December 2025

Bolivia eliminated fuel subsidies in December 2025, causing gasoline and diesel prices to increase by nearly 200%, shifting from state-controlled prices of about 53 cents per liter to market rates. The Central Obrera Boliviana (the country's largest labor federation) organized mass protests. At least four law enforcement officers were injured during clashes with police. Studies showed an average 19% decrease in presidential approval following subsidy removal in Bolivia.

## Angola — July 2025

Angola raised diesel prices by 33% as part of an IMF and World Bank-supported subsidy removal program in late July 2025. Transport costs subsequently increased by 55%. At least 30 people were killed (documented by Human Rights Watch), over 1,500 detained, and police used excessive force against peaceful protesters.

Context: Unemployment stood at 14.5%, inflation at 27.5%, wages were stagnant, and half the population lived on approximately $2 daily. Angola's 2025 debt obligations totaled $58.6 billion (63% of GDP). Social services spending had been reduced over 55% since 2015.

Civil society organizations noted that fuel subsidies represented "a lifeline for half of Angola's population" and removal before adequate social safety nets risked escalating unrest.

## France — 2018 Yellow Vest Crisis

President Macron's fuel tax increase triggered the Yellow Vest movement, causing a significant drop in presidential approval. This demonstrated that fuel price sensitivity and political backlash is not unique to developing countries.
