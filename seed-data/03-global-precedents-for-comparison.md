# Global Precedents: What Happened When Other Governments Cut Fuel Subsidies

## Cases Where Subsidy Removal Triggered Political Crisis

### Nigeria 2012 — "Occupy Nigeria"
- Government abruptly removed fuel subsidy on January 1, 2012 with no warning
- Fuel price doubled overnight (65 to 141 Naira/liter)
- Mass protests erupted in every major city within 48 hours
- National general strike paralyzed the economy for over a week
- 11 protesters killed by security forces
- Social media (Facebook, Twitter) played central role in coordination
- Government forced into partial reversal within 16 days
- President's credibility severely damaged

### Nigeria 2023 — Tinubu's Removal
- President Tinubu ended subsidies on inauguration day (May 29, 2023)
- Petrol price increased 300% to 650 Naira/liter
- 73% of Nigerians dissatisfied with the manner of removal
- Inflation reached 40%; food prices up 150% annually
- Major multinationals (GSK, P&G, Shoprite) exited the country
- Hunger protests erupted in August 2024 across all major cities
- Analysts warned of "regime change" risk similar to Bangladesh

### Indonesia 1998 — Fall of Soeharto
- 71% gasoline price increase during Asian financial crisis
- Violent riots lasted weeks across the country
- President Soeharto forced to resign after 31 years in power
- Demonstrated that fuel price changes can topple governments

### Bolivia 2025 — 200% Price Shock
- Fuel subsidies eliminated entirely in December 2025
- Gasoline and diesel prices increased nearly 200%
- Largest labor federation organized mass protests
- Presidential approval dropped by average 19%

### Angola 2025 — Deadly Crackdown
- Diesel prices raised 33% in July 2025 (IMF/World Bank backed)
- Transport costs up 55%
- At least 30 killed, 1,500 detained
- Half the population lives on $2/day — no safety net in place

## Cases Where Reform Was Managed Successfully

### Indonesia 2022 — Jokowi's Phased Approach
- 30% fuel price increase announced
- Simultaneous direct cash transfers to 20+ million households
- Wage subsidies for low-income earners
- Protests occurred but were manageable
- Key factor: Jokowi was in his final term (no re-election pressure)

### India — Gradual Deregulation
- Diesel prices deregulated in 2014
- Petrol prices had been deregulated since 2010
- Phased approach over years minimized shock
- Rising economy helped absorb price changes
- Strong political mandate for Modi government

### Egypt — IMF-Backed Reform
- Phased subsidy reduction over 3 years (2014-2017)
- Cash transfer program (Takaful and Karama) expanded simultaneously
- Inflation spike but manageable
- Strong government control limited protest movement

## Key Patterns Relevant to Malaysia

### What Triggers Violent Backlash:
1. **Abrupt removal** without warning or transition (Nigeria 2012, Bolivia 2025)
2. **No compensatory mechanism** in place before removal (Angola 2025)
3. **Large price shock** — increases above 50% are particularly dangerous
4. **High existing economic stress** (unemployment, inflation)
5. **Low trust in government** and perception of corruption
6. **Strong organized opposition** with mobilization capacity

### What Enables Successful Reform:
1. **Phased implementation** over months or years
2. **Pre-existing cash transfers** that people already receive and trust
3. **Final-term leaders** with no re-election pressure
4. **Clear communication** about where savings go
5. **Visible investment** in public services using savings
6. **Moderate price increase** (under 30%) that doesn't shock household budgets
7. **Strong economic growth** that gives people alternatives

### Malaysia's Position (Assessment):
- Malaysia has implemented BUDI95 (targeted subsidy, not full removal) — moderate approach
- SARA cash transfers are already in place
- Public trust in Anwar is moderate but not high
- Vehicle dependency is extreme (34 million vehicles, 20% public transport usage)
- Opposition (PN/PAS) has strong grassroots mobilization capacity
- Next general election creates political pressure
- Rising global oil prices threaten fiscal sustainability of current approach
- If forced to further cut or remove RON95 subsidy, risk level is MODERATE to HIGH
