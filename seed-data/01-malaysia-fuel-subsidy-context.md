# Malaysia Fuel Subsidy Reform: Current State and Historical Context

## Current Fuel Subsidy Structure (as of March 2026)

Malaysia maintains one of the cheapest fuel prices globally:
- RON95 petrol: RM1.99/liter (subsidized via BUDI95 program) — 10th cheapest globally
- RON97 petrol: RM3.16/liter (unsubsidized, market rate)
- Diesel (Peninsular): RM2.15/liter (subsidized for transport sector)
- World average petrol price: US$1.30/liter

The price gap between subsidized RON95 (RM1.99) and unsubsidized RON97 (RM3.16) is RM1.17 — creating massive market distortions.

## Fiscal Cost

- Total annual subsidy bill: RM81 billion (fuel, food, utilities combined)
- RON95 subsidy alone: approximately RM20 billion annually
- Current monthly subsidy cost for RON95: approximately RM3.2 billion (as of early 2026)
- Estimated fuel consumed by foreigners: RM3-4 billion annually
- Oil/gas sector contribution to government revenue fell from 35% in 2010 to 20% in 2024

The BUDI95 targeted subsidy program (launched September 2025) saved approximately RM2.5 billion annually by removing subsidies for top-income earners and foreigners. Non-subsidized RON95 for foreigners and high-income Malaysians costs RM2.56/liter.

## Vehicle Ownership and Transport

Malaysia has exceptionally high vehicle dependency:
- 17.2 million registered cars
- 16.8 million registered motorcycles
- Public transport usage: only 20% of population
- This makes fuel price changes directly impact virtually every Malaysian household

## The BUDI95 Mechanism

Launched September 22, 2025, BUDI95 is Malaysia's targeted RON95 subsidy:
- Malaysian citizens with valid MyKad and driving licence receive subsidized RON95 at RM1.99/liter
- Monthly quota: 300 liters per person
- Non-citizens and top 5% income earners pay market rate (RM2.56/liter)
- Uses MyKad biometric scanning at fuel stations for verification

## Timeline of Malaysia's Subsidy Reform Journey

**2008**: PM Abdullah Badawi raised diesel (+40%) and petrol (+67%), triggering massive public backlash that contributed to his political downfall.

**2018**: Mahathir's government floated RON97 while maintaining RON95 subsidy — targeting luxury vehicle owners only.

**October 2023**: Budget 2024 revealed RM81 billion total subsidy bill. PADU (Central Database Hub) launched for citizen income profiling.

**January-March 2024**: PADU mass registration yielded only 17.65 million accounts out of 30.4 million target profiles — significant data gaps.

**June 2024**: Diesel subsidy removed in Peninsular Malaysia (RM2.15 to RM3.55, a 56% increase). Retained in Sabah/Sarawak and for fishermen. RM200 monthly "Budi Madani" cash transfer introduced for eligible households. Subsidy removal saved RM600 million/month largely through reduced smuggling.

**July 2024**: Unity Government lost Sungai Bakap by-election, attributed partly to public anger over diesel subsidy removal.

**October 2024**: Budget 2025 signaled RON95 reform targeting the top 15% income earners (T15) and foreigners.

**May 2025**: PM Anwar narrowed the target to top 5% (T5) and foreigners only — backing away from the broader T15 scope after sensing political risk.

**July 23, 2025**: PM Anwar announced "People Appreciation" package including RON95 price cut from RM2.05 to RM1.99 — a populist move before implementing the subsidy restructuring.

**September 22, 2025**: BUDI95 officially launched with MyKad-based verification system.

**October 2025**: Budget 2026 revealed that targeted subsidy measures saved the nation RM15.5 billion annually across all subsidy categories. Additional RM100 SARA cash handout announced, funded by subsidy savings.

**March 2026**: Cabinet confirms RON95 subsidized price maintained at RM1.99. Monthly subsidy cost has risen to RM3.2 billion due to rising global oil prices — a 90% increase from the RM600 million/month baseline. Government faces renewed questions about fiscal sustainability.

## Key Vulnerabilities

1. Malaysia's RM3.2 billion monthly subsidy cost is rising and eroding fiscal savings
2. The RM1.86 price gap between RON95 and RON97 incentivizes all users (including wealthy) to use subsidized fuel
3. The 300-liter monthly quota is generous enough that most users never hit the cap
4. MyKad verification creates friction at fuel stations and excludes some legitimate users
5. Only 20% public transport usage means there is no viable alternative for most Malaysians
6. Rising global oil prices could make the current subsidy level unsustainable
